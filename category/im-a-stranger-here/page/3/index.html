Advertising

I’d just left the family and was spending my Sunday afternoon passing though SeaTac security. When I saw *it*. Advertising. On the bottoms of the trays we have to put our laptops, jackets, belts, doggie-bags of liquids, shoes and underwear in. Someone probably voted for the “impression count”(§) model of advertising as opposed to the “positive association”() approach.

It always seems surprising to me where adverts will appear, and it also saddens me that deep down, they are effective.

(I’m typing this up later, and the transit security in Copenhagen didn’t have adverts on their trays. Indeed the whole austere-white/grey of European airports have obvious, but minimal ads in general.)

Back to the SeaTac experience; I won’t be buying *it*, whatever it was. Unless I do it later subconsciously.

—

§ Impressions is where a blanket approach to advertising an offer, offers or brand affects you a significant time later when making product choice. It is blanket advertising in terms of flyers through doors, repeat adverts in newspapers and magazines and ‘pack-ins’ in boxes of things you have ordered from other companies – e.g. the flyers in an Amazon.com box. I believe in advertising the number of times you see something is the “impression count” even if you *discount the actual advert* (aka ‘Comcast sucks’/’I’m already with T-Mobile’). So the relevance and inconvenience is not related to the usefulness of the actual ad per se. If you’ve seen six dozen AT&T ads for various things in the last year, you will at least *know* the products AT&T sells when you need to make your next product decision.

 James Bond wears an Omega watch, I want to buy one, because then I’ll be like James Bond. I think the association with a British Secret Service assassin is positive. Because he’s cool. And wears an Omega.

American Retail

Stretching the Price, originally uploaded by machaggis.

While it lasts you can go to the Sony website and look at this offer for the Sony Reader (and the small print), but as it won’t last I added this screenshot.

I’ve been intrigued by ebook readers (especially eInk ones) for a while now – basically every time I travel and have to lug a book or two, or stop halfway through a mammoth (think Steven Erickson) and pick up a small paperback. Although both the Kindle and the Reader with their eInk technology have intrigued me also. I was a sucker for the iPod too.

Two things have stopped me:

a) DRM and pricing on the books (they’re as expensive, if not more, than the paper version despite being cheaper) and they’re generally tied to some crazy “you don’t really own the encrypted pile of bits” DRM system

b) price of the bl**dy readers, but I’m intrigued enough that I watch the prices and look for a bargain….

Point (b) neatly leads me to the real object of my pontification: prices in America and how it is strange.

i) the price you see (everywhere except gas) does not include sales tax (VAT for the Europeans). So that $1 MacDonalds – really $1.08. That $16,000 Volkswagen Beetle – really $17,482. More than a little annoying. It also changes every 100 yards as cities, boroughs, states, and counties call impose their own piece of sales tax (e.g. the proposed “tourist hotel sales tax” for Seattle). It makes calculating tips hard too, as you don’t know what you’re tipping on. However it makes going to VAT-inclusive countries fun, as everything seems a little cheaper and equally strange – “a 34c cake costs 34c? how quaint!”

ii) rebates – super common, you pay $2,500 for your MacBook Pro with “free printer” only to actually pay $100 for the printer (which is of course, $109 after sales tax, and then you actually bought the slightly-better $125 printer anyway). You then cut various parts off the boxes of both the computer and the printer and send them away. You pay the credit card bill, so you are now out the cash for the printer. 6 weeks later a check arrives the for “free printer”. For $100. As they don’t compensate for sales tax, or the sneaky upgrade the salesman sold you on. Grrrrr. I hate rebates(*). An American thing as far as I know – it’s the manufacturer bunging the discount, and doing it this way ensure a) traceability and b) a lot of non-compliance which makes you money and c) causes the retailer grief which d) the retailer can pass on to the customer rather than managing the discounts as coupons (i.e. discounts at the till). For a while, when I worked at Amazon our Head of Retail was European, and he also hated rebates. He tried to kill them. He couldn’t due to the infrastructure of American retail. He also tried to simplify “cash back” (spend $399 in kitchens and receive $50 sometime later in life unless you violate the really small print). Also failure.

iii) “value pricing” – especially in outlet malls. A pair of jeans $30 with “$50 value”, so a $20 saving. Really? So why aren’t they ever priced at $50? I believe the whole “sale pricing laws” in the EU prevent this misleading bollocks.

iv) Flat out lying. Ahhh, the subject of our Sony advert, so let’s drop out of bullet points into analysis.

What is the big red number in this picture? $108.99. How much is the Reader? $349.99 they would lead you to believe.

How much do you pay?

Who the f*ck knows!!!! (Answer may be below).

Let’s deconstruct….

There is a pretty straightforward $50 discount leading to $299.99 – so that’s a good guess.

There is free shipping until Thursday, so the nominal “Express Shipping $17” is represented a saving, except it’s not a saving on the $299 as it would be added on anyway. So that’s bad math.

There’s a “New Sony Card Member” saving. It only works if you don’t have a Sony credit card and apply for one. And then (if my quick read of the print is right) if you spend $299 or more (whew, that $299.99 seems good now) and then it arrives with a credit of $150 you can spend. But not on the Reader. As your initial purchase can’t be a qualifying purchase. But that seems simply wrong, so I’ll give the small print scan the benefit of the doubt. But this $150, is that cash in the bank or a saving on the Reader? WTFK?

And then there is the $24 savings that comes from the points (8 points per $1) that you gain by buying (the presumably $299) Reader with the card (assuming its issued to you straight away). This is like saying a $400 airline ticket is only $300 because you earn airmiles worth a potential $100 ticket to Taipei (at 3am, one day only, during the monsoon).

And the $199 worth of 100 cheap ass $1.99 books that are all/mostly free online anyway that you have to have downloaded (all of them) by September 30th. And they’ll have DRM.

So, how much do you pay? I’m thinking something like $327 ($299 plus that ever elusive sales tax).

And you won’t have to pay shipping (which is free on the whole store anyways).

However, you also get another credit card.

With $150 on it.

And 30 days to download a pile of cheap books.

And maybe $24 of points (do you have to use them on Sony stuff? – I’m not sure).

And it doesn’t work on a Mac (but that’s nothing to do with the pricing).

This is not $108.99.

This is what I mean about lying about price.

(*) And of course, if you also bought a MacBook with the same offer at the same time (because both your computers had been stolen), you end up only getting one $100 check because, of course, the small print says 1 offer per household, even though you bought two computers and the guy in the Apple Store gave you two rebate coupons for two free computers.

The US Farm Bill

Similar to the crazy EU farmer subsidies (suck it up Frenchies), things are not all well in the US of A:

A public-health researcher from Mars might legitimately wonder why a nation faced with what its surgeon general has called an epidemic of obesity would at the same time be in the business of subsidizing the production of high-fructose corn syrup.

And the ties to other parts of state/federal-subsidized behaviour is concerning:

a school lunch lady trying to prepare healthful fresh food is apt to get dinged by U.S.D.A. inspectors for failing to serve enough calories; if she dishes up a lunch that includes chicken nuggets and Tater Tots, however, the inspector smiles and the reimbursements flow

When Danika was at school, ketchup counted as the ‘vegetable’. (Makes my lunch money forays to the Garrell Vale Leisure Centre for a roll-and-chips seems healthy – at least I exercised and had no pretensions of vegetable intake.)

The farm bill essentially treats our children as a human Disposall for all the unhealthful calories that the farm bill has encouraged American farmers to overproduce.

What is this all about? Another great NYT times article “You Are What You Grow“. I think I may need to subscribe just to make sure it never goes away (to be honest, that’s the principle behind my National Geographc subscription too – mostly unread).

At least in the US there is some consumer backlash:

it was that consumer, after all, who built a $15 billion organic-food industry and more than doubled the number of farmers markets in the last few years

But I doubt those consumers were evangelical gun-lovers from the mid-west, so I don’t see politics changing soon.

Mmmm, was that too cynical? I don’t think so – as soon as the Le Tour is over I’m sure there will be another strike in France.
