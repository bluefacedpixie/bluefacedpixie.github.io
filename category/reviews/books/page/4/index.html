Garfield – minus Garfield

I loved Garfield in my late teens/twenties. And still have a softspot for it as a cartoon – right there with Calvin and Hobbes (and now being a father, I dread the concept of Calvin as much as I adore the idea of an imagination such as portrayed in that strip).

But who knew that Garfield was making such a deep statement on life on the sly? The blog Garfield minus Garfield shows strips with Garfield removed. The result is a portrayal of Jon, and life, as a disturbing unsettling place. As the blog intro says (with my spelling corrections):

Who would have guessed that when you remove Garfield from the Garfield comic strips, the result is an even better comic about schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and the empty desperation of modern life?
Friends, meet Jon Arbuckle. LetÂ’s laugh and learn with him on a journey deep into the tortured mind of an isolated young everyman as he fights a losing battle against loneliness and methamphetamine addiction in a quiet American suburb.

Surreal is the word that leaps to my mind.

Book: Deadhouse Gates

Deadhouse Gates by Steven Erikson.

A mammoth (600+ page) book gifted from my wishlist by kind relatives, along with other books in the Erikson series.

Deadhouse Gates is #2, although only related by setting, a few characters and some interlinks with #1 Gardens of the Moon. As premodernist mentions later in the series, the books are basically good, well designed fantasy.

I must write a few detractions: the stories occasionally veer into the needlessly gory (blood everywhere) and painfully gross (crucifixion). I think Erikson does this to make the books adult and also real and he deals with endless wars and doesn’t want to overplay any perspective of ‘glory’, but really – sawing a head off with a chain in the prologue is just blatantly unnecessary. And there is more like that. I think he’s also trying to set the books in a ‘goth’ scene (lots of desiccated skin, Clive Barker effects etc) but the rest of his writing is basically above it and as time goes on the transition to the ‘gross part’ seems to become more unnatural and breaks the flow – at other times he writes sensitively and by implication (on Wickan sorcery for instance). Another detraction is that he pulls a lot of rabbits out of a lot of hats. I cannot decide whether he’s making these saving graces up as he goes along or whether it’s a part of his well-established world – with authors you never know. And finally, his big improvement over many other big fantasy books is his ‘everyman’ perspective – many characters are the people on the ground, not princes and kings etc. However I’m becoming less convinced this is honest – in this book warlords seem to be Ascending, our favourite assassin is becoming more “the best assassin who ever lived since the God of Assassins” and the scurvy mages are actually really *much better than they appear*.

Regardless of this I’ve worked my way through the book over the past few weeks reading each page diligently – there is a wealth of detail and you *will* be punished if you skim-read (be prepared to flick back) and the story is so engaging I finally bulldozed through the last 150 pages on Wednesday night, heading to bed on Thursday. And it was worth it – even if half of the interleaved storyline is battle after battle after battle. The next book is Memories of Ice, which I already have on my shelf. But I’ll wait a while, to digest and read other things (Freakonomics, Ruby on Rails, Passage to Juneau) before returning the worlds of sword and sorcery.

Book: Woken Furies

I’m just coming out of a massive sci-fi book kick that I haven’t been sharing. I picked up a bundle of books to take to Mexico a month ago, some long-awaiting sci-fi series and by chance (as choice was dictated by the second hand book store) some other easy-to-reads that all ended up as sci-fi. I immediately returned from vacation only to find that the Woken Furies (Richard Morgan) hardcover had arrived in the library. More sci-fi.

I’ve consumed this over the last week or so – and it’s really good, bloody noir sci-fi with Takeshi Kovacs, ex-Envoy, gun for hire, ex-criminal, confused psycho, once more the anti-hero. The only issue here is that he becomes more anti-hero and less sympathetic to the reader in every book. In Altered Carbon he was the flawed action figure and the sci-fi, near-future universe of the Protectorate was being established. In Broken Angels, Kovacs was more than a little psychotic, but we were off-planet, the space opera was pretty encompassing and there were Martians. It’s really hard to tell which of those books were the best – they were both 4’s in their own way.

Woken Furies isn’t quite so good – the setting is established and Morgan pushes it as far as it can go. Kovacs is basically now mental and it’s hard to care for him, and the book reads like an endless series of set-pieces with a few particularly gory fight scenes. Not that it isn’t good – if you only read one near-future sci-fi this year this would not be a bad choice. But the warning signs were there – the blurb on the backcover seems more enamoured with the fact that Altered Carbon has been optioned by Joel Silver than the story within or the author.

But, 3/5 in the grand scheme of things.

Book: The Charnel Prince

I’m going to start with the negative. The Charnel Prince suffers from the persistent problem with series fantasy these days: the books are weighty, the cast is wide, and who remembers who’s who when you’re reading the sequel a year after the first volume (The Briar King). Please, please, please, please do a cast of characters (at least George RR Martin does this) or a synopsis. Please.

Now, the good stuff. The book was great, I loved reading it, the story has some corny-ness but is overall good, and the chapter-per-character format so often used these days keeps the pages flicking by. Three new characters enter the fray (a composer, a tracker from a village and a mysterious saviour) and the conspiracy and intrigue continue to grow.

The main hero characters (Anne, Neil, Aspar) continue their heroic exploits with a good support. To say much more would give away some story, and you can always read the synopsis at Amazon.

I’m tempted with the 5, but will rest easy with a very conservative 4/5.

“A Wounded Mastodon” (Book: Big Weather)

Martin thinks the Robert Jordan series

rolls on like a wounded mastodon down a gentle slope

I think he hasn’t read Big Weather, which in one book wants to make you suffocate yourself by swallowing a whole honeydew melon and gives a whole new meaning to speed reading – every page has the same word on it somewhere (“tornado”). It would have (and I think it did) made a good 3000-word article for Outside magazine – endlessly repeating with no panache for 300 pages was just no fun – weather is not that exciting. Unless you’re talking about the recent Seattle rain. No, I was wrong, that’s boring too.

Big Weather – 1/5.

Book: Pompeii

I actually finished Pompeii by Robert Harris during the holidays but didn’t get a chance to blog about it.

Quite simply it’s 4/5, especially if you like any other Robert Harris or want to read a nice historical fiction novel that isn’t an endless fantasy series. The setting feels pretty authentic (other Robert Harris books are more contemporary but also more clearly adjusted for dramatic effect) and although there are apparently real historical figures cast I’ve no idea what liberties have been taken with them.

Basically there is the aqueduct, a mountain about the blow up (but everyone thinks the gods have indigestion) and a small town called Pompeii, which should really know better. Add in a little bit of double-cross and thriller-type action and it’s a nice tight story (I don’t want to spoil too much). It does make you realise that the Romans were pretty good engineers.

Harry Potter and the Half-Blood Prince

As Danika already blabbed, I rode the bus to work for the whole week so that I could read Half-Blood Prince. It was close call yesterday when our friend Kristen got on the same bus, and then I had Date Night after work, but I managed to finish it off before midnight.

I’m not a rabid fan, but my gut feeling is this is the best yet. Although Order Of The Phoenix was probably a slighly better story, the Half-Blood Prince was better edited and (for adults) more funny (although the fun is mostly about teenage relationships rather than the kid jokes). Very little can be said about the book without spoilers, so I’ll summarize: tightly written/edited, not overly long for no reason (the print is big), fairly consistent and fun story (although drops the DA element of last book strangely), good character development, not a _naff_ ending. Definitely worth bussing it for a week (especially given the fortunate turn of crappy weather Seattle gifted us with ðŸ™‚ ).

If it takes two years to have #7 with the same level of quality, then let’s wait (well George RR Martin just made us procrastinate forever for A Feast for Crows and that’s a book that ended up split in 2!).

For my next trick I am going to stick with fiction, as I’m reading too rarely to do a work book or non-fiction just now. Probably Pompeii by Robert Harris.

The score on the door for Half-Blood Prince? 4 out of 5.

I’ve just finished reading “How Mumbo Jumbo Conquered The World” by Francis Wheen, which is a cutting non-fiction political book about how things have changed in the last twenty-five years in a rather _post-Enlightenment_ way. It was a birthday present a few months ago and after a long hike up Mt. Si today with my friend Paul, who teaches History on the east coast, I have decided to pass it along as it makes for good quotes and a different perspective on life. Even if the politics don’t matter the random facts are great.

The ‘quintessentially British’ meal of fish and chips owed its existence to Sir Walter Raleigh, who brought potatoes back from Virginia in the 1580s; it has in turn yielded place to the nation’s new favourite, chicken tikka massala, an Anglo-Indian hybrid. Meanwhile, India may have become the world’s largest producer, consumer and exporter of chillis, but the plant itself is native to Mexico: it was introduced to Europe by the Spanish in 1514 and then to Asia by Portuguese seafarers a century later. How many Indians today regard the chilli as a symbol of Euro-American hegemony?

And I always thought the story of pizza was interesting.
