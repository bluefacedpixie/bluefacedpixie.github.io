Letting off steam

How not to design a website.

expedia.com. One of the world’s most popular travel websites.

I asked “….but i couldn’t see a way to specify a manual car and see the rates….”

The answer is, I think, “don’t ask us”. Seriously. At least that’s what the customer service reply seems to indicate (my bold). It’s either that or a really, really bad non-answer.

Manual transmission cars are available. Here’s how to search for rental cars and make reservations:

1. Go to the Expedia.com home page, http://www.expedia.com, and click the blue “Cars” tab at the top.

(If you have already have set up flight arrangements, access your itinerary — via the “My Itineraries” link at the top of any Expedia page — and click on the “Add car rental” link.)

2. In section No. 1, “Where do you want to pick up your car?” select one of the three choices: “At an airport,” “Near a Place,” or “Near a US address.” Then enter the pickup location and date. For one way rentals, check the box to indicate “My drop-off location is different from pick-up location.”

3. In sections 2-4, enter your car-class preference, discount code (if applicable), and car-company preference.

4. If you need special equipment, such as a ski rack, car seat, or car with left-hand control, click the link titled “Need special equipment?”

5. Click the green “Search” button.

6. Make your selection from the options shown by clicking the link titled “Verify rate and continue.” During the purchase process, you’ll be able to see details on the rate, specify the driver, and enter information on frequent-traveler programs.

If you have further questions regarding this issue, feel free to reply to this e-mail or contact Expedia customer services at 1-800-397-3342 and reference case ID 40856292. You can also visit the Expedia.com “Customer Support” page (http://www.expedia.com/daily/service/default.asp) for more customer service information.

Thank you for choosing Expedia.com.

Glad to see their customer service instructions reflect the realities of their website.

Needless to say I had already booked the crappy automatic Ford Focus, basically because their site was cheapest overall.

Here are a couple of snippets, on raising fuel prices:

The more likely outcome of higher energy prices is a move to alternative energy sources which offer a lower cost, even if doing so results in more pollution instead of less. An example would be the wood burning stove boom during the 1970s oil crisis or waste oil burners.

And on an oblique reference to those fellows in Greenpeace (bless their cotton socks):

Simply attacking an environmentally damaging activity is not effective unless a better alternative of similar or better economics and usefulness is presented

American Retail

Stretching the Price, originally uploaded by machaggis.

While it lasts you can go to the Sony website and look at this offer for the Sony Reader (and the small print), but as it won’t last I added this screenshot.

I’ve been intrigued by ebook readers (especially eInk ones) for a while now – basically every time I travel and have to lug a book or two, or stop halfway through a mammoth (think Steven Erickson) and pick up a small paperback. Although both the Kindle and the Reader with their eInk technology have intrigued me also. I was a sucker for the iPod too.

Two things have stopped me:

a) DRM and pricing on the books (they’re as expensive, if not more, than the paper version despite being cheaper) and they’re generally tied to some crazy “you don’t really own the encrypted pile of bits” DRM system

b) price of the bl**dy readers, but I’m intrigued enough that I watch the prices and look for a bargain….

Point (b) neatly leads me to the real object of my pontification: prices in America and how it is strange.

i) the price you see (everywhere except gas) does not include sales tax (VAT for the Europeans). So that $1 MacDonalds – really $1.08. That $16,000 Volkswagen Beetle – really $17,482. More than a little annoying. It also changes every 100 yards as cities, boroughs, states, and counties call impose their own piece of sales tax (e.g. the proposed “tourist hotel sales tax” for Seattle). It makes calculating tips hard too, as you don’t know what you’re tipping on. However it makes going to VAT-inclusive countries fun, as everything seems a little cheaper and equally strange – “a 34c cake costs 34c? how quaint!”

ii) rebates – super common, you pay $2,500 for your MacBook Pro with “free printer” only to actually pay $100 for the printer (which is of course, $109 after sales tax, and then you actually bought the slightly-better $125 printer anyway). You then cut various parts off the boxes of both the computer and the printer and send them away. You pay the credit card bill, so you are now out the cash for the printer. 6 weeks later a check arrives the for “free printer”. For $100. As they don’t compensate for sales tax, or the sneaky upgrade the salesman sold you on. Grrrrr. I hate rebates(*). An American thing as far as I know – it’s the manufacturer bunging the discount, and doing it this way ensure a) traceability and b) a lot of non-compliance which makes you money and c) causes the retailer grief which d) the retailer can pass on to the customer rather than managing the discounts as coupons (i.e. discounts at the till). For a while, when I worked at Amazon our Head of Retail was European, and he also hated rebates. He tried to kill them. He couldn’t due to the infrastructure of American retail. He also tried to simplify “cash back” (spend $399 in kitchens and receive $50 sometime later in life unless you violate the really small print). Also failure.

iii) “value pricing” – especially in outlet malls. A pair of jeans $30 with “$50 value”, so a $20 saving. Really? So why aren’t they ever priced at $50? I believe the whole “sale pricing laws” in the EU prevent this misleading bollocks.

iv) Flat out lying. Ahhh, the subject of our Sony advert, so let’s drop out of bullet points into analysis.

What is the big red number in this picture? $108.99. How much is the Reader? $349.99 they would lead you to believe.

How much do you pay?

Who the f*ck knows!!!! (Answer may be below).

Let’s deconstruct….

There is a pretty straightforward $50 discount leading to $299.99 – so that’s a good guess.

There is free shipping until Thursday, so the nominal “Express Shipping $17” is represented a saving, except it’s not a saving on the $299 as it would be added on anyway. So that’s bad math.

There’s a “New Sony Card Member” saving. It only works if you don’t have a Sony credit card and apply for one. And then (if my quick read of the print is right) if you spend $299 or more (whew, that $299.99 seems good now) and then it arrives with a credit of $150 you can spend. But not on the Reader. As your initial purchase can’t be a qualifying purchase. But that seems simply wrong, so I’ll give the small print scan the benefit of the doubt. But this $150, is that cash in the bank or a saving on the Reader? WTFK?

And then there is the $24 savings that comes from the points (8 points per $1) that you gain by buying (the presumably $299) Reader with the card (assuming its issued to you straight away). This is like saying a $400 airline ticket is only $300 because you earn airmiles worth a potential $100 ticket to Taipei (at 3am, one day only, during the monsoon).

And the $199 worth of 100 cheap ass $1.99 books that are all/mostly free online anyway that you have to have downloaded (all of them) by September 30th. And they’ll have DRM.

So, how much do you pay? I’m thinking something like $327 ($299 plus that ever elusive sales tax).

And you won’t have to pay shipping (which is free on the whole store anyways).

However, you also get another credit card.

With $150 on it.

And 30 days to download a pile of cheap books.

And maybe $24 of points (do you have to use them on Sony stuff? – I’m not sure).

And it doesn’t work on a Mac (but that’s nothing to do with the pricing).

This is not $108.99.

This is what I mean about lying about price.

(*) And of course, if you also bought a MacBook with the same offer at the same time (because both your computers had been stolen), you end up only getting one $100 check because, of course, the small print says 1 offer per household, even though you bought two computers and the guy in the Apple Store gave you two rebate coupons for two free computers.

Stupid Ass Rating Systems

So Cork’d (which I don’t use) has changed it’s stunningly obvious 5-star rating system to a 100-point scale.

Not in itself stupid ass. However when we investigate the “universally accepted 100-point rating system” we discover it actually only goes from 50, increases on this scale 50/60/70/80/85/90/92/96/99 (although you are allowed to use the points in between) and rates the bottom of those point ranges thusly:

* 60-69 Points

Flawed wines to avoid at all costs. Practically undrinkable.

* 50-59 Points

So poor that the 60-69 range is insufficient to describe their inadequacy. Undrinkable.

So basically the 100 points are liberally wasted with everything below 70 being crap, and oodles of oenophiles bitching about subtle one-point distinctions from 90 upwards (and it’s almost a complete stupid ass in itself that the 90 range goes 90-91, 92-95, 96-98, 99-100; what’s with that whole 4 point wedge at 92-95?).

I like my rating system, but am not wed to it. One wine website has a like it/don’t like it voting system – simplicity.

This 100-point system? a) not really a 100 point system more like a 31 point system; b) stupid ass.

We are the mass extinction event, originally uploaded by machaggis.

Elephants are increasingly threatened by human intrusion. Between 1970 and 1989, the African elephant population plunged from 1.3 million to about 600,000; the current population is estimated to be between 400,000 and 660,000.

Painting on Christmas Eve

See also I hate painting.

I love living in my little house in Ballard, I like the way it looks. I often complain about yard work, which I have little skill for and don’t really get fulfillment from (except in a “I moved the dirt” kind of way, and I may be the only person who likes mowing small lawns).

Painting/decorating is different. In general I like the sense of achievement from the finished product – the red living room, the new furniture etc. But I hate the act of painting. It’s tedious, and I like to be good at stuff and I’m not very good at painting. Bad edges, splashes, squiggling lines. All of these irritate me and I have to ignore them at the end of the day to appreciate the results. This time round I even had to drywall for the first time – buying random things and asking naive non-macho-American questions at Home Depot. Doesn’t look like I’m more than passably bad at that either.

However I spent the part of Christmas Eve that wasn’t at the ER finishing drywall and painting our upstairs lobby (white, with some river rock “tough finish” greenish to go on the stairway on Boxing Day) and I think I’m going to be happy. We’ve already moved upstairs to the green room (skylights installed by contractor-man, touch-up by me last weekend) and Baby MacHaggis will live upstairs in the yellow room. I think this is called familial domesticity, and it’s definitely not software-working, blog-writing virtual.

Merry Christmas tomorrow, I hope Santa is on his way and that you’ve been mostly good all year.

Becoming the enemy….

I’m developing some issues with etiquette, and for once a 5 minute browse of the internet just isn’t helping.

It’s becoming all-out war at our favourite weekend bistro – Besalu – for tables.

Now that the weather has turned (or the flood has started, depending on your perception of Fall in Seattle this year) the outside tables are no longer useful for the café. This means that the small number of tables inside are ever so in demand. Besalu is popular, and we go most weekends at varying times between 8 and 9.30 am. A line out of the door with people waiting to buy excellent pastries and good coffee is not unusual, and although many of these customers get “to-go” we’re normally in the group that would like to eat on location.

And that is the problem, because there are two types of sit-in customer – the ones (like me) who wait in line, and when they are about to be served assess the table situation and order “for-here” or “to-go” appropriately, and then the self-serving righteous crew who hawk tables constantly while they are in line and immediately place a “keeper” (such as a book or a jacket) when a table empties and then return to their place in line. They have effectively reserved a table, regardless of how many people ahead of them in line would like to sit down but have not aggressively seized their spot.

This has been happening a lot the past few weeks, and this morning I even challenged the person who came in after us (one middle-aged, middle-classed Seattle-ite) who promptly placed a notepad on a table for three (the only free table) and took her place in line behind the 32-week pregnant Danika. “It’s common practise”, she answered. “It’s very rude”, I replied (and bit my tongue on what I wanted to say).

So, we are now at the zero-sum game of mutually assured destruction. After raging internally about this all morning I have decided that my moral sense of fairness is eroded to the point that I will become the enemy. I want that table in Besalu, but faced with an environment where there is no social contract or mannered-etiquette that allocates tables on a first-come-first-served, and I will start doing exactly what I despise. Just like the prisoner who always end up ratting out on their buddies in a Prisoner’s Dilemma, I’ll be taking my magazines, hats and gloves to café, skipping line and stealing tables from grannies, toddlers and pregnant women. So there. Welcome to Seattle; we often pretend we’re as polite as Canadians.

(I reserve the right to return to normally mild-mannered, morally outraged, nicely brought-up Scotsman at any time.)
