Making a disastrous photo passable

It happens all the time – especially in our house with our couch nicely in front of the window – a photo has light behind it and the subjects are in shadow unless you use that nasty flash (ok, sometimes flash is fine).

I take photos in RAW (actually Nikon’s RAW which is called NEF) format which makes for big files that it’s very easy to tweak afterwards.

I use Adobe Lightroom 2 in a very trivial way to manage my photos (rather than iPhoto for example) because I have a reasonable number of photos and Lightroom is built to manage that and RAW files in particular (so is Apple’s Aperture, but the trial version ran slow and I got a line on discounted Lightroom – the end).

I don’t do much “Photoshopping” but here is a quick 15 second walkthrough of how I a) rescued a cute picture from last weekend to something looking like a passable family snapsot and b) added a cool effect that makes it look fun!

2008 Activity Summary

A very belated, and somewhat truncated, summary of my 2008 sporting activity courtesy of some spreadsheet crunching this weekend.

In general, up from 2007, but as 1/4 of 2007 was untracked/Lucified I don’t think it’s that much of a difference.

My days of doing nothing are down to about 131, which I am happy about, although my mountain biking continues to suffer – work commuting and running count for most activities (and as I trained for and ran an ultra-marathon relay with Ragnar in 2008 the running part isn’t surprising).

A Very American Peculiarity

12th February 1809.

I meant to post something about this a few weeks ago, when the world was celebrating the 200th birthday of Darwin, and the US was somewhat celebrating that and the 200th birthday of Abe (now that’s a co-incidence, even if Newsweek thinks its a footnote).

There are a few things I don’t understand about the US, even as I draw towards being here for a decade. And this one stumps me. Europe, with it’s history of blood, religion and persecution, accepts the science and reconciles itself to Evolution (ok, there may be some mistakes and fakes, but whatever…). America, the country that is so scientific it has sent people to the moon, split the atom, cured cancer, doesn’t. And some States don’t want to teach it, or also want to teach it with “Intelligent Design” etc. Although changes are being made there:

A federal judge has ruled that intelligent design is a religious view that does not belong in a science classroom. The ruling comes as school boards and legislatures across the country debate how to teach students about the origins of life on Earth.

So the Darwin reflections turn, once more, into debate.

I consider this state of affairs a Very American Peculiarity.

Watchmen – not a review

My friend Morgan and I are getting frustrated. There’s this big comic-book movie out this week – Watchmen, based on a seminal graphic novel from 23 years ago now that is a little dated (remember the Cold War?) and morally ambiguous. But today’s entertainment is all morally ambiguous, so it’s not that different.

But the problem is, we can’t work out if the movie’s a good big-screen blast. We like to go watch big action movies in the Cinerama. It’s great. It almost makes bad movies good.

All the movie reviews are quagmire’s of comparison of the movie to the comic for comic-nerds, or how its not relevant anymore, etc, etc (e.g.). I want to know if I should go watch it. I probably will.
