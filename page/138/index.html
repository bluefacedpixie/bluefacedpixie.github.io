1,500 miles

Synchronicity.

Danika and I were both discussing separate articles (me – an NPR broadcast on a couple of Canadians, Danika – an article in Eating Well) about how the food a US resident eats travels an average of 1,500 miles. I repeat *1,500* miles.

I was astounded. I’ve often wondered at the ridicule of buying a $9 Old Navy t-shirt made with US cotton manufactured in Asia (i.e. think how many round trip miles that is, _and it is still profitable, *big time*, for Old Navy etc_). And don’t even think of WalMart and Target and…. And I never thought to think of it in terms of food, although I always enjoyed those _Egyptian_ off-season potatoes in Scotland.

Anyway, turns out this isn’t new – a quick google gives me a 1995 article of how the CIA were worrried about the US food supply lines when the food only had to travel 1,400 miles.

My story is that the two Canadians were trying to eat produce grown within 100 miles for a year. They were surviving quite well, good beef, fish, vegetables, wine, many potatoes. But no wheat, beer or anything as extravagant as coffee or tea. And to be a vegetarian without custom beans such as tofu was hard. They also did a lot of freezing and drying for the off-season. Danika’s article talked about restaurants doing the same thing but I can’t imagine paying $$ to eat frozen food in January (although I probably do without realizing it).

So – environmental and ecological and self-sustainability and social economic issues aside – this Christmas Day (or whatever politically correct term you wish to use for the 25th), here is the challenge. For every ingredient, think how far it has traveled. You don’t even have to tell me, but there is a comments section.

Merry Christmas (bah humbug to political correctness).

Narnia: The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe

On a lazy pre-Christmas afternoon we went to see The Chronicles of Narnia: The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe last Sunday.

In general both Danika and I were unimpressed. The child actors were good, but not at the level of sophistication of the Harry Potter crew (although better than those actors at the beginning), the battle scenes were uninspiring (and not bloody in order to get a child-friendly rating) and overall the movie lacked drive. I don’t think it’s a spoiler for anyone that _Aslan dies_ and this is meant to be upsetting etc. Instead you’re left fidgeting wondering when they’ll cut to the next scene, and is it really PC to have two girls so weepy?

We still don’t really get the attempts to re-write the Christian mythology – these stories are about sorceries and talking animals for crying out loud. And if every story that has a main character die and return has to be interpreted in that manner I’d rather it wasn’t one where they waffle about the “deeper magic”.

On the upside though it looked pretty and timing was very good – although over two hours it didn’t feel excessively long despite the lack of engagement. And the “old house in the country” stuff was very English literature, and the evacuation of the London children was filmed very well.

Overall I have to say *2/5* merely because I have no desire to see it again but I would recommend it as an entertaining distraction on a big screen with good sound and as a Christmas movie it’s pretty good – after all Narnia has gone 100 years in ice with no Christmas. The return of St. Nick is long overdue.

The many names of Mud

A few weeks ago I kitted my cycle-to-work bike up with thin knobbly tires and stripped it of all commuting niceties (water bottle, lights, little bag with puncture kit etc) and headed out to try my hand at cyclocross for the first time.

A few weeks ago I kitted my cycle-to-work bike up with thin knobbly tires and stripped it of all commuting niceties (water bottle, lights, little bag with puncture kit etc) and headed out to try my hand at cyclocross for the first time.

Cyclocross is a sport I’d been wanting to do since coming to the North-West in 2000 but due to distractions, lack of knowing anyone else and the fact that Oct/Nov/Dec is the busiest time at work for me I had never made it.

Cyclocross is riding a race across fields and through mud using a bike more like a road bike than a mountain bike – no big suspension or powerful brakes. It has a very loyal following, is a trendy growing sport in this part of the world and has created a categorization of mud – my race had an extensive section of Goo and some patches of Slop.

I did OK for my first ride out at Donida Farms (a tough course with no respite) although I was pretty low in my age group rankings. As I’m not really a racer at heart I’m not sure I’ll be doing more than the occasional race in the future (the current season is already finished), but it sure was fun – and riding a bike over rough ground with no suspension was very 1970’s.
