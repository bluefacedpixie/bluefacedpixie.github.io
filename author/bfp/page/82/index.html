Book: Deadhouse Gates

Deadhouse Gates by Steven Erikson.

A mammoth (600+ page) book gifted from my wishlist by kind relatives, along with other books in the Erikson series.

Deadhouse Gates is #2, although only related by setting, a few characters and some interlinks with #1 Gardens of the Moon. As premodernist mentions later in the series, the books are basically good, well designed fantasy.

I must write a few detractions: the stories occasionally veer into the needlessly gory (blood everywhere) and painfully gross (crucifixion). I think Erikson does this to make the books adult and also real and he deals with endless wars and doesn’t want to overplay any perspective of ‘glory’, but really – sawing a head off with a chain in the prologue is just blatantly unnecessary. And there is more like that. I think he’s also trying to set the books in a ‘goth’ scene (lots of desiccated skin, Clive Barker effects etc) but the rest of his writing is basically above it and as time goes on the transition to the ‘gross part’ seems to become more unnatural and breaks the flow – at other times he writes sensitively and by implication (on Wickan sorcery for instance). Another detraction is that he pulls a lot of rabbits out of a lot of hats. I cannot decide whether he’s making these saving graces up as he goes along or whether it’s a part of his well-established world – with authors you never know. And finally, his big improvement over many other big fantasy books is his ‘everyman’ perspective – many characters are the people on the ground, not princes and kings etc. However I’m becoming less convinced this is honest – in this book warlords seem to be Ascending, our favourite assassin is becoming more “the best assassin who ever lived since the God of Assassins” and the scurvy mages are actually really *much better than they appear*.

Regardless of this I’ve worked my way through the book over the past few weeks reading each page diligently – there is a wealth of detail and you *will* be punished if you skim-read (be prepared to flick back) and the story is so engaging I finally bulldozed through the last 150 pages on Wednesday night, heading to bed on Thursday. And it was worth it – even if half of the interleaved storyline is battle after battle after battle. The next book is Memories of Ice, which I already have on my shelf. But I’ll wait a while, to digest and read other things (Freakonomics, Ruby on Rails, Passage to Juneau) before returning the worlds of sword and sorcery.

Find Prime Items on Amazon.com

I have Amazon Prime – an $80 a year addiction that allows most Amazon things to be sent to you with 2-day shipping without paying for the shipping (‘coz you’ve paid $80 for the privilege).

I’m pretty sure it costs Amazon money because Danika and I regularly buy any crap we think of so we don’t have to go into the real world and shop. We also buy soo many presents and ship them 2-day because that way they get there for the event – rather than the cheapskate free shipping offer we used to use which had a 3-9 day delivery window. Amazon hopes to make money on the loyalty and all the profit on the items purchased.

So what? Well the problem is that Prime only works on Amazon items and Amazon sells their own items and other people’s and they don’t apply. Because Amazon search sucks (a well accepted fact) you can’t use Amazon to find items that can only be shipped by Prime.

Which brings us to the heart of the matter. Due to the wonders of the internet and webservices, some kind soul brings us: the Amazon.com Prime Items Finder. It really needs a better name – like Heaven Sent.

I am a little suspicious the kind soul may be sticking his associate tags on the clickthroughs though – I would.

Oh, kaching, I think I’ve just spent more money at Amazon.

RSS!

I was just about to repost that if you hadn’t read my old how-to-use-RSS post you should, as RSS is saving my butt at keeping up with learning about mobile stuff at my new job.

More importantly though, the folks at bloglines have just tweaked their UI and it looks a little nicer, works better and is oh-so trendy, more Web 2.0. And then I see this review that bloglines sucks compared to Google Reader. And Google Reader used to be really bad.

So there are now two good web-based readers, and there are many applications for both Windows and Mac. So there is no excuse not to be reading bluefacedpixie.com and planetdanika.com and lots of other great sites: on RSS!
