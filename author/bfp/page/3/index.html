25th February 2003 – Hamilton, New Zealand

We had hooked up with my friend Cameron (from a mountain bike trip in Peru) in Australia, but apart from that it had been Scotland when we had last interacted, in person, with anyone we knew. Now in our last days in NZ this started to change. After finishing our Abel Tasman hike we headed north once more, caught a repeat of an LOTR movie, completely loved the art deco in Hastings etc and went to visit Gary who was on sabbatical from the University of Zambia to University of Waikato (ne Hamilton). Gary is an old friend from the days of CompSci and stuck with the whole academia gig while I slogged on with corporate capitalism. Oh yeah, and it started to rain again…

21st February 2003 – Bark Bay Hut, Abel Tasman, New Zealand

As a Scotsman (Glasgow = 49″ of rain a year (one inch less than NYC)) and a resident of Seattle (37.5″ of rain a year, but let’s not talk about the clouds) I really shouldn’t be *that* annoyed that we had a week or two of rain-drenched adventure. After all New Zealand is a not disimilar temperate climate; it’s a long thin island in a big ocean full of clouds; it’s very *green* which means it must *rain* but after our awesome Kepler Track hike we paid our dues. Running from rain in the south glaciers we headed to Arthur’s Pass (Pop. 54) only to be flooded out again – our new tent was not a boat!!

But we carried on, with panache.

Day 229 finds us bunking in bunk beds, wet, after arriving late, on the very awesome Abel Tasman Track in NZ. It’s a strange country where it has a bundle of great hikes, it advertizes them and people (and tourists!) hike them. And yet they are not overcrowded or subject to lottery etc (I’m looking at you Mt. Rainier; Alpine Lakes et al). The advantages of a great ratio of population:awesomeness (the US has this as well but it’s so dang large that the ratio of local population:local awesomeness sometimes overwhelms the awesomeness). And yes, I use the word awesome a lot now.

But 229 days into the trip, as our last newsletter notes, we were starting to think of THE END. The clock was ticking. The USA was approaching. Our time in NZ, and Australia, and parts beyond, was starting to become very, very finite.

(On a side note, I have no recollection of “Puzzle World in Wanaka” and need to talk to Danika about that when she gets off an awful I99N snarl up)

Wetter than a Scottish summer. – 19th February 2003

(Being a re-post of a ten-year old Yahoo groups newsletter update)

[Boots-Off Internet, Nelson, New Zealand]

We’ve spent the last week hiking our butts off and getting completely soaked, though not at the same time. We’ve experienced our worst weather yet in New Zealand and discovered that our new tent is NOT waterproof from below when sitting in a field three inches deep in storm rain! The rain has put a little bit of a dint in our plans: hopes to take a helicopter ride and full-on glacier hike were ditched, and we took rather a large detour to a place called ‘Arthur’s Pass’ only to have to leave again due to the blasted rain 🙂 Fortunately, the rain has ceased for the time being, and we managed to do some fun stuff before and during!

Last week we went on a cruise and kayak in the Milford Sound fiord, we overnighted on the boat and the difference between the windy and rainy evening and the blue-sky morning was a wonder to see. We followed this by tackling our first more-than-one-day “Great Walk” in NZ – the Kepler Track. It took us four days (though we could have done it quicker) and was quite spectacular. There were fiords, alpine crossings, mountain peaks (Mt. Luxmore) and enchanting forests. We had to carry food and big packs, but stayed in excellent NZ huts so we didn’t have to carry the big tent! We befriended quite a few characters on the walk and even managed to watch some beach cricket. All in all, we thought it was such great fun that we’re going to do another Great Walk tomorrow. This time, we’re doing the Abel Tasman track which is mostly coastal and includes lots of beaches and some beach crossings at low tide only! We plan to do this walk a little faster – just two days, just as well as our ferry leaves the South Island the same day we finish the track!

After the beauty of the Fiordland area we drove up the west coast in some damp and wild weather. Although this put paid to our planned ‘treat’ of a helicopter hike on the Franz Josef or Fox glaciers we did go for a guided hike on the Franz Josef glacier anyway. This was good fun, despite a little rain (snow would have looked nicer) and was not near as cold as you may have thought! That night was when the storm rain started that plagued us for the next few days, but last night we finally spent a dry night in the tent and woke up to sunshine and clear skies.

We’re chilling in Nelson – fully drying out from recent days and catching up on a few chores. Saturday night, we return to the North Island for a last few days before we head back to Australia. We’re considering this the start of the ‘journey home’, after a weekend in Australia we start the several-day trek to Buffalo where we’ll consider the main part of the trip over. We’re happy and sad to be considering our return – we’re not really done travelling but unless we want to do a lot of fruit-picking, our time and budget is up!

Here’s to being in Buffalo in two weeks!

Danika & Gordon

This week in New Zealand was very much a Lord of the Rings tour although the two days nominally “not a main sight” were spent in Te Anau which ranks as one of my favourite named towns in the whole wide world. “Ben Lomond is my nemesis” – 10th January 2003

(Being a re-post of a ten-year old Yahoo groups newsletter update)

[Internet Laundromat, Te Anau, New Zealand]

“Ben Lomond is my nemesis” was what Danika was feeling the day before yesterday as we hiked up the Ben Lomond in Queenstown. This mountain is higher, steeper and more difficult than the Ben Lomond that we hiked in Scotland a few months ago. But we made it to the top despite that!

We’re now in the South Island of New Zealand. Since we emailed last we’ve hiked the fabulous day walk “Tongariro Crossing” (Mordor) which was excellent, driven down to Wellington and taken the ferry across the Cook Strait (accompanied by a few shy dolphins) to the South Island.

We’ve found that the South Island is bigger and more remote than we expected – we seem to have spent a lot of time driving about. We’ve been to the low-key and cute ‘cities’ of Christchurch and Dunedin (both far too cold to consider a permanent base) and driven to the picturesque Mt Cook region where we did a little hiking.

After that we headed towards Queenstown, the Adventure and Adrenalin Capital of New Zealand. On the way to Queenstown we stopped off in Alexandra in Central Otago where Gordon went mountain biking. It was good fun (he skipped most of the extreme stuff) and was very similar to East Washington or North California – strange to be so far away but so similar! After going to Queenstown Danika jumped off a bridge with a little bit of elastic attached to her ankles.

Yup!!! Danika Bungy-jumped from Kawarau Bridge!!! It’s totally incredible and she still can’t quite believe she did it – but if you’re going to do it, you might as well do it in Queenstown.

Now we’re down in beautiful Fiordland hanging out in Te Anau. In the next week we’ve got an overnight trip on Milford Sound and a large 4-day hike in the Kepler mountains to look forward to. Then we start to work away back up New Zealand on the long trek home.

Hope all is well with everyone,

Gordon and Danika
